Briefkist 1.0 — your mail stays home, now with a spare key we never hold

07 Jul 2026 · Niels Filmer · 6 min read

Two years ago this project was a Mac mini in a cupboard and a working name that turned out to belong to someone else. Today it is Briefkist: the same private, local-first archive for the paper mail of a household — and, for the first time, a way to have it without owning a server.

The principle hasn't moved a centimeter. A letter is photographed on your phone, read and understood by AI on a single machine, and filed into an archive that answers questions like "the letter about the chimney inspection". No cloud, no telemetry, no third party in the data path.

What's new

The server is now open source, AGPL-3.0, in the open on GitHub. Self-hosting is and will remain the full product — nothing is paywalled, nothing phones home.

And for households without a spare computer or the appetite to run one: we will now run a single-tenant server for you, in the EU, for €12 a month. Everything it stores is encrypted with a key derived from a passphrase only you hold — we keep only ciphertext. We wrote down exactly what that does and doesn't protect against on the security page, including the words we refuse to use for it.

A privacy product that can't tell you its weakest moment doesn't know its own strength. Ours is the seconds a letter spends in RAM being read — and we put that in the marketing.

What's next

Honest comparisons, starting with "Briefkist vs Paperless-ngx" and "when NOT to use Briefkist" — because a tool for trust should be easy to rule out. If you've been running the beta at home: thank you, and your archive migrates in place.
